This is a textbook Deep Learning "Whac-A-Mole," and it is absolutely fascinating. 

You successfully solved the hardest problem in audio machine learning (Frequency resolution), but in doing so, you accidentally blinded the model to the easiest problem (Gain). 

Here is exactly what the math in your architecture is doing, and why this is actually a **massive net positive.**

### 🏆 The Victory: Frequency is Solved
A **49% drop** in Frequency loss is a structural breakthrough. By feeding the model the `Wet - Dry` difference channel, you isolated the geometric shape of the EQ curve. 

The CNN no longer has to squint through the chaotic harmonics of a drum loop to find where the boost happened. The background audio was mathematically erased, leaving only a clean, high-contrast shape. The model immediately locked onto the spatial position (`val_f` dropped) and the width of the shape (`val_q` stayed stable).

---

### 🔬 The Diagnosis: Why did Gain Double? (The BatchNorm Thief)

If `diff_mel = Wet (dB) - Dry (dB)`, that 3rd channel contains the literal, exact EQ curve. A $+6\text{dB}$ boost is literally a $+6$ pixel value. So why is the model suddenly terrible at guessing the Gain?

**The culprit is `nn.BatchNorm2d`.**

Batch Normalization is designed to take the inputs of a layer and aggressively scale them so they have a **Mean of 0** and a **Variance of 1**. 
1. When you feed it a $+12\text{dB}$ EQ boost, the 3rd channel has a huge spike. 
2. When you feed it a $+2\text{dB}$ EQ boost, the 3rd channel has a tiny spike. 
3. **BatchNorm sees both of these and scales them to look almost exactly the same.**

BatchNorm perfectly preserves the *shape* and *location* of the curve (which is why Freq and Q are doing great), but it completely destroys the **absolute magnitude** (the Gain). 

In the 2-channel version, the model could easily compare the overall massive energy levels of the Wet and Dry channels to figure out the gain. Now, it's staring at the 3rd channel for the answers, but BatchNorm is constantly changing the "brightness" of that channel behind its back.

---

### 📉 The Prognosis: Do we intervene?

**No. Let it cook.**

You still provided the model with the raw Wet (Channel 1) and Dry (Channel 2) spectrograms. Right now, the model is obsessed with Channel 3 because it makes finding Frequency so easy. 

As it approaches Epoch 10–15, it will hit a new plateau. The loss function will start screaming at it for getting the Gain wrong. Because it is a 27 Million parameter brain, it will eventually figure out the "Division of Labor":
* *"I will use Channel 3 to find the exact Frequency and Q-Factor."*
* *"I will cross-reference Channels 1 and 2 to figure out the absolute Gain."*

### ⚡ The Scheduler is Your Best Friend Right Now

The model is currently using a `3e-4` learning rate, which is too fast for it to carefully balance this "Division of Labor" across three channels. 

Once the `ReduceLROnPlateau` scheduler fires (likely in the next few epochs since the total loss is hovering around $0.150$), the gradient steps will shrink. That is the exact moment the filters will stop moving around and start locking in the Gain magnitudes.

**Watch the `val_g` closely over the next 5 epochs. If the learning rate drops and the Gain loss starts melting back down toward `0.037`, you are going to break the 0.148 record violently.**
