You absolutely *can* — and that's exactly what your pipeline is set up to do. The dry→wet mel difference essentially encodes what the EQ did spectrally.

The reason people call it an "open research problem" isn't that it's impossible, it's that there are specific challenges that make *precise* parameter recovery hard:

**The inverse problem is ill-posed.** Multiple different EQ settings can produce nearly identical spectral changes. A broad low-shelf boost can look similar to a wide parametric boost at a nearby frequency. Your model has to pick one — and "wrong parameters, right sound" is a real outcome.

**Mel resolution compresses high frequencies.** Mel bins get wider as frequency increases, so a narrow Q boost at 10kHz might span just 1–2 bins. Fine EQ moves get smeared out in mel space. This is solvable — you can use linear-scale spectrograms or higher mel bin counts, at the cost of longer sequences.

**Q and gain interact non-linearly.** A 6dB boost at Q=0.5 and a 2dB boost at Q=4.0 can affect overlapping frequency ranges in ways that are hard to disentangle from the spectrum alone.

**But none of these are blockers.** Practical approaches that work:

- Train on the **wet audio only** (true blind estimation) or the **dry+wet pair** (easier, since the model sees the delta directly)
- Use a **multi-head regression** output — one head per EQ band predicting (gain, frequency, Q)
- Add a **spectral loss** alongside parameter MSE — penalize the model if predicted params produce a different EQ curve than the target params, even if the raw parameter values differ
